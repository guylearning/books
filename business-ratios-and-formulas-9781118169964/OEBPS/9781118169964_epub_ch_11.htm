CHAPTER ELEVEN

Measurements for the Human Resources Department

THIS CHAPTER FOCUSES ON the measurements that can be used to determine the performance of the human resources department. The primary focus of the measurements described here is the department's ability to efficiently hire employees. Also included is employee turnover, which is perhaps the most popular employee-related measurement in use today.

The measurements discussed in this chapter include:

Employee Turnover
Average Time to Hire
Late Personnel Requisitions Ratio
Sendouts per Hire		 Intern Hiring Percentage
Ratio of Support Staff to Total Staff
Employment Cost Effectiveness

AVERAGE TIME TO HIRE

Description: A key function of the human resources department is its ability to hire staff within a reasonable time. This can be a difficult and lengthy process that is also subject to the opinions of the interviewers, thereby making it an even longer process that is not entirely under the control of the human resources department. It is possible, however, to judge the recruiting performance of the human resources department to some extent by using a measurement of the average time required to hire employees.

Cautions: There are a number of problems with this measurement. First, the human resources manager may mandate the hiring of lower-quality employees to drive down the time to hire. Second, the measure includes only those job postings for which the search has been completed; incomplete searches, which may have been open for many months, are not included at all. Third, department managers may continually reject candidates located by the human resources manager, which increases the length of the time to hire while not giving the human resources manager any real control over shortening the results. Thus, this measure should not be used as the sole criterion for human resources performance; using it in combination with other measures provides a better overall view of departmental performance.

SENDOUTS PER HIRE

Description: Many companies use outside recruiting firms as part of their systems for hiring new employees. It is common enough to judge recruiters based on the fees they charge, but this takes no account of their efficiency in sending out to the company those individuals most likely to be hired. If a recruiter is sending out a flood of applicants, then the recruiter is not spending enough time screening them, or does not have an adequate knowledge of what the company wants. The sendouts per hire metric is useful for tracking this type of efficiency.

Formula: Divide the number of sendouts into the number of hires from the group sent to the company by the recruiter. It is best to calculate sendouts per hire over a large number of positions, since there may be anomalies related to individual positions that do not realistically reflect on the performance of the recruiter.

Example: Wilco Electronics needs to hire a group of designers for its new series of ham radios that are powerful enough to communicate with orbiting astronauts. Cautions: Sendouts per hire can vary substantially by the type of technical training required, geographic region, how well the company has defined positions for recruiters to act upon, and the pickiness of the individuals at the company making the final hiring decisions. Thus, the sendout ratio may be quite high if a recruiting firm is filling janitorial positions, since the hiring standards for such positions are low. Conversely, a high-end programming position may require the services of a person so difficult to find that the number of sendouts per hire may be extremely large.

EMPLOYMENT COST EFFECTIVENESS

Description: A local government may be interested in issuing tax credits to a business to persuade it to locate within the geographical area administered by the government entity on the grounds that the business will hire local residents. It may be worthwhile, however, to examine the employment cost effectiveness of the business before offering any tax credits. Some companies are very capital intensive and so give the appearance of being capable of hiring, and yet do not do so, either as a result of automation or because they outsource labor-intensive tasks to suppliers.

Example: Zippee Backup operates an online backup system for the consumer market. It wants to open a data facility in Billings, Montana, but wants a $5 million, 10-year interest-free loan from the municipal government in exchange for doing so. The controller of Billings calculates the employment cost effectiveness of Zippee and learns that Zippee only generates one job per $250,000 invested in it. This is because of the high infrastructure cost of Zippee facilities. The government does not see a good reason for extending the loan when there is such a low probability of increased hiring by the company, based on historical evidence.

Cautions: This measure is derived from historical information, and so may not be a good indicator of future performance. Nonetheless, a company that is currently capital-intensive is likely to remain that way. Conversely, if a business has a poor employment cost effectiveness ratio due to its outsourcing practices, those practices could potentially be reversed, which could substantially alter the ratio in the future.
