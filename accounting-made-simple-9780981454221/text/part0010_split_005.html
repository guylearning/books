Current vs. Long-Term

Often, the assets and liabilities on a balance sheet will be broken down into current assets (or liabilities) and long-term assets (or liabilities). Current assets are those that are expected to be converted into cash within 12 months or less. Typical current assets include Accounts Receivable, Cash, and Inventory.

Everything that isn’t a current asset is, by default, a long-term asset (a.k.a. non-current asset). For example, Property, Plant, and Equipment is a long-term asset account.

Current liabilities are those that will need to be paid off within 12 months or less. The most common example of a current liability is Accounts Payable. Notes Payable that are paid off over a period of time are split up on the balance sheet so that the next 12 months’ payments are shown as a current liability, while the remainder of the note is shown as a long-term liability.
